02 Problem Discovery

How I found the problem

It wasn't a complaint we received. It was a structural ceiling we saw coming.

The trigger

As P2P trading volume grew, CS ticket volume scaled with it — linearly, and without any natural resolution mechanism. Every dispute, regardless of size or complexity, followed the same path: one CS agent, manual back-and-forth with both parties, email threads, individual clarification calls, approval chains. The smallest payment confirmation dispute took the same minimum 2+ days to reach first response as a high-value fraud escalation.

We projected forward. At the growth rate we were seeing in P2P adoption, CS capacity would become the hard ceiling on how much volume the platform could responsibly handle. The choice was binary: hire aggressively into CS (expensive, slow, and brittle), or build a resolution infrastructure that resolved disputes without CS involvement wherever possible.

"10–12% of P2P transactions triggered a dispute. Every single one landed in the same queue. CS couldn't triage by severity — there was no mechanism to do so."
P2P platform pattern — Crypto exchanges
Established P2P exchanges (LocalBitcoins, Binance P2P) report that 8–15% of trades result in some form of dispute or appeal. The majority are resolved at the counterparty level when a structured communication channel exists.
Interpretation: Our 10–12% dispute rate was within normal range. The problem wasn't dispute frequency — it was the total absence of a self-resolution layer.
Internal CS data — Pre-launch baseline
All disputes routed directly to CS via ticket. Minimum 2 days to first response. Post-response, each dispute required multiple back-and-forth cycles — individual outreach to buyer, individual outreach to seller, clarification loops, approval by risk profile, then fund release. No differentiation between a £20 payment confirmation and a £2,000 fraud escalation.
Interpretation: The system was optimised for nothing. No triage, no self-resolution, no fast path for simple disputes. CS time was the only resource — and it was being consumed uniformly regardless of dispute value or complexity.
Capacity modelling
At projected P2P volume growth, CS headcount required to maintain existing SLAs would need to increase 3–4x within 18 months. Cost per dispute resolution was trending upward as CS time was fixed and dispute volume was not.
Interpretation: Hiring was not a strategy — it was a delay. The only durable solution was infrastructure that could resolve disputes without CS involvement for the majority of cases.
The validated pattern

P2P platforms cannot scale dispute volume on CS headcount alone. Without a structured, tiered resolution mechanism — self-resolution first, CS second, platform arbitration last — every new P2P trade is a potential CS ticket. Trust in a P2P marketplace is not built through policies. It is built through infrastructure that users can see working in real time.

The problem statement

P2P traders at Coinhaven who enter a dispute have no mechanism to communicate with their counterparty, no visibility into resolution status, no path to self-resolution, and no differentiation by risk level. Every dispute — regardless of size, complexity, or user trust level — is processed identically through a CS queue with a minimum 2-day wait. This is not a support problem. It is a trust infrastructure gap that makes scaling P2P volume operationally impossible.

06 The Solution — Design Decisions

Cleared — every decision has a reason

None of these were defaults. Each design choice was made against an explicit alternative that was considered and rejected.

Decision
Three-tier escalation, not direct CS routing
Why: Routing all disputes to CS immediately was the status quo — and it was the ceiling. The first design decision was to introduce a counterparty chat layer (T1) where the majority of disputes — payment confirmations, timing delays, receipt disputes — could self-resolve without any CS involvement. CS capacity was freed for the disputes that actually needed it. T3 (platform arbitration) was reserved for edge cases only: high-risk profile involvement or deadlocked T2 cases. In practice, T3 was rare.
Decision
Funds held in escrow throughout the dispute lifecycle
Why: The alternative was to hold funds only if CS flagged the trade. This was rejected immediately. Escrow protection had to be automatic on dispute initiation — not discretionary. A dispute raised by either party triggered an immediate fund block. This removed the incentive for a bad actor to pressure the counterparty outside the platform, because neither party could access the funds regardless of the other's behaviour. Release required explicit agreement at the resolution tier reached.
Decision
Self-resolution trigger: mutual agreement in T1 chat → automatic fund release
Why: Manual release approval for every resolved dispute would have put CS back in the loop even for disputes they never needed to touch. If both counterparties confirmed resolution within the T1 chat, funds were released automatically. This was the mechanism that made T1 meaningful — not just a communication channel, but a resolution pathway with an actual outcome. It required trusting the counterparty interaction log as the source of truth, which in turn drove the design of the in-platform chat (timestamped, immutable, visible to CS in case of escalation).
Decision
24h user-triggered CS escalation, not automatic timer
Why: We considered auto-escalating to T2 after 24h regardless of counterparty chat status. This was rejected because it would flood CS with disputes still in active dialogue. Instead, the user could request CS escalation at any point if they felt T1 was deadlocked — and the system auto-triggered T2 if 24 hours elapsed with no resolution and no escalation request. This kept the escalation user-driven first, and time-bounded second. It also gave CS a clean signal: any T2 ticket meant T1 had genuinely failed.
Decision
Resolution authority and trade limits linked to KYC risk profile — not uniform across all users
Why: Treating a fully verified Tier 3 user identically to an unverified Tier 1 user in a high-value dispute was both a trust failure and a risk failure. The risk profiling engine already governed trade amounts and transaction limits. Dispute resolution was extended into the same framework: the level of KYC verification determined what a user could approve (fund release at T1), what required CS countersignature, and what required CEO/founder-level sign-off (high-risk profile, large fund settlement). This was not a separate system — it was an extension of the trust architecture already in place.
Decision
Repeat dispute actors flagged and risk-profile downgraded, not individually managed
Why: A user who initiated multiple disputes — particularly disputes that resolved against them — should face lower trade limits and higher scrutiny, not a one-off case review each time. The system was designed to feed dispute outcomes back into the risk engine: repeat flagged actors moved to lower tiers, reducing their trade limits and transaction approvals. In the most severe cases, accounts were blocked and funds frozen pending review. This closed the loop between dispute behaviour and platform access — making abuse of the dispute system structurally costly.
07 System Mechanics

How the tiers worked

Each tier had a defined scope, time boundary, resolution authority, and fund release condition. The system was designed so that each escalation left a complete evidence trail for the next tier.

Tier 1
Counterparty Chat
0 – 24h · Immediate resolution
Trigger: Either party raises a dispute on a matched P2P trade
Fund status: Automatically blocked in escrow on dispute initiation
Channel: In-platform timestamped chat between buyer and seller only
Resolution: Mutual agreement in chat → automatic fund release. No CS involved.
Escalation: User-requested at any point, or auto-triggered after 24h with no resolution
Covers: ~90% of disputes — payment confirmations, receipt verification, timing delays
Tier 2
CS Review
24 – 48h from escalation
Trigger: User request or 24h T1 expiry with no mutual resolution
CS access: Full T1 chat log, trade details, both user risk profiles, KYC tier, transaction history
CS action: Reviews evidence, contacts both parties within the platform, makes a resolution recommendation
Fund release: CS-approved release once a resolution is confirmed. Risk-profile determines whether CS can release unilaterally or requires countersignature.
Escalation: Deadlocked T2 or high-risk profile involvement → T3
Tier 3
Platform Arbitration
36 – 72h · Edge cases only
Trigger: T2 deadlock, high-risk profile on either side, or large fund settlement requiring elevated approval
Decision authority: CEO / founder-level involvement for highest-severity cases. Platform makes a binding decision.
Fund release: Platform-decided — can release to either party, split, or freeze pending further investigation
Outcome: Binding. Results fed back to risk engine — dispute actors flagged, trade limits adjusted, accounts blocked if warranted.
Frequency: Rare by design. T1 self-resolution absorbed the majority of volume; T3 existed for genuine edge cases.
08 Success Metrics

How we knew it was working

Four metrics. Each tied to a specific behaviour the system was designed to move. If a metric didn't change a decision, it wasn't tracked.

90%
CS deflection rate
Post-launch, CS involvement dropped to 10% of disputes. ~60% reduction in dispute-related support queries overall. The majority of disputes were resolved at T1 — between counterparties — without any CS touch.
–2 days
Minimum resolution time
Before: all disputes required a minimum 2-day wait before CS could even begin working on them. After: T1 disputes resolved immediately on mutual agreement. T2 CS response: 24–48h. The 2-day baseline became the upper bound for the most common tier, not the floor for all disputes.
Tiered
Resolution time by tier
T1 self-resolution: immediate upon agreement. T2 CS resolution: 24–48h from escalation. T3 platform arbitration: 36–72h, required only in rare edge cases. The system collapsed time for the majority while preserving capacity for complexity.
Zero
Production incidents on launch
Cleared shipped as part of the broader P2P release — one of 4 major platform releases delivered on time with zero production incidents across a platform processing 1K+ daily transactions.
Guardrails — these must not move in the wrong direction
Fund release accuracy: no disputed funds released to the wrong party. Any mis-release is a critical incident regardless of amount.
T1 abuse rate: users gaming the dispute system to delay legitimate trades must not exceed a threshold that undermines counterparty trust in P2P.
CS workload: T2 volume must not increase as a % of total disputes post-launch. If CS is handling more disputes proportionally, T1 is not working as designed.
Risk profile integrity: risk tier downgrades for repeat dispute actors must be reviewed quarterly to ensure the signal remains accurate and isn't producing false positives against legitimate traders.
09 Pre-Mortem

What could go wrong

Assume Cleared ships. Six months later it is pulled or rolled back. What happened?

🐯 Tiger — real problem
Dispute system used as a delay mechanism
A bad actor raises a dispute on a legitimate trade — not because there's a real problem, but to hold up fund release while they execute another transaction. T1 gives them a 24h window before CS is even triggered. At scale, this becomes a systematic exploit.
Mitigation: Dispute frequency monitoring per user. Repeat dispute actors — especially those whose disputes consistently resolve against them — are flagged in the risk engine and their trade limits reduced. Accounts with patterns of bad-faith disputes are blocked. The risk profile feedback loop makes abuse structurally costly.
🐯 Tiger — real problem
T1 chat becomes evidence-fabrication ground
A counterparty uses the T1 chat to create a misleading paper trail — selectively sharing information to manipulate CS when T2 is triggered. Without immutable logging, CS sees a version of events curated by the bad actor.
Mitigation: T1 chat is timestamped, immutable, and fully visible to CS at T2. Neither party can edit or delete messages. The chat log is the source of truth for all escalation decisions. CS also has access to the full transaction history and KYC profile — not just the chat.
🐘 Elephant — unspoken risk
High-volume sellers lose confidence in platform fairness
If Tier 3 users — the high-KYC, high-volume traders who create P2P liquidity — feel the dispute system is biased against sellers or that CS decisions are opaque, they exit quietly. No complaint. Just reduced volume, then departure.
Mitigation: T3 decisions are documented with reasoning. Both parties receive a decision summary — not just an outcome. High-tier users have access to founder-level escalation, which is both a trust signal and a structural check on CS decision quality. Monitoring T3 repeat actors from the same side (buyer or seller) is an early warning signal.
🐯 Tiger — manageable
Escrow hold damages trust more than the dispute itself
A user whose funds are correctly escrowed during a legitimate delay — not a real dispute — perceives the platform as the problem. The escrow mechanism designed to protect them feels like the platform is blocking their money.
Mitigation: Escrow UI must communicate clearly: "Your funds are protected — not frozen." Status updates at each tier change. Estimated resolution time shown at T1 and T2. The experience of fund protection must feel like safety, not punishment.
10 What I'd Validate Next

The questions shipping would answer — and what we built after

These were the open questions at launch. Several became features in subsequent iterations.

Fraud pattern detection at the dispute level
The risk engine governed trade limits and KYC tiers. But it did not yet analyse dispute-level patterns — specific transaction types, counterparty combinations, or payment methods that correlated with bad-faith disputes. The next layer was building a fraud signal at the dispute level: what does a weaponised dispute look like before CS notices? This became a subsequent build, layering dispute behaviour signals into the risk profiling engine that already governed the platform.
Automated resolution for low-value disputes
Not all disputes needed a human in the loop. A payment confirmation dispute on a £15 trade, between two Tier 3 KYC-verified users, with a matching transaction reference — this could be auto-resolved with high confidence. The question was: what is the right value threshold, the right risk profile combination, and the right evidence signal to trigger automated release without CS? Defining those parameters safely — and building the automated path — was validated and shipped as a subsequent feature.
Seller reputation scoring
P2P liquidity depends on seller reliability. A buyer choosing between two sellers with similar offers has no signal beyond the transaction. Dispute history — how often a seller is disputed, how disputes resolve, response time in T1 chat — is exactly the data that would power a reputation layer. This was identified at launch as the next trust-building feature. A seller's dispute record would surface as a lightweight score visible at the point of trade matching, giving buyers a signal and incentivising sellers to resolve at T1.
T1 resolution rate by dispute type
We tracked CS deflection in aggregate. The next question was: which categories of dispute consistently fail at T1 and escalate to T2? If payment confirmation disputes self-resolve at 95% but "goods not received" escalate at 80%, those two dispute types require different T1 tooling — perhaps structured evidence templates rather than open chat, or a shorter auto-escalation window. Segmenting resolution rate by dispute type was the data foundation for making T1 smarter.
