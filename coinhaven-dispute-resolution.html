02 Problem Discovery

How I found the problem

It wasn't a complaint we received. It was a structural ceiling we saw coming.

The trigger

As P2P trading volume grew, CS ticket volume scaled with it — linearly, and without any resolution mechanism. Every dispute, regardless of size or complexity, followed the same path: one CS agent, manual outreach to both parties, email threads, clarification cycles, approval chains based on risk profile, then fund release. The smallest payment confirmation dispute took the same minimum 2-day wait to first response as a high-value fraud escalation.

We projected forward. At the growth rate we were seeing in P2P adoption, CS capacity would become the hard ceiling on volume the platform could responsibly handle. The choice was binary: hire aggressively into CS — expensive, slow, and brittle at scale — or build resolution infrastructure that could handle disputes without CS involvement wherever possible.

"10–12% of P2P transactions triggered a dispute. Every one landed in the same queue. CS had no way to triage by severity — there was no mechanism to do so."
P2P platform benchmarks — Crypto exchanges
Established P2P exchanges report 8–15% of trades resulting in some form of dispute or appeal. The majority are resolved at counterparty level when a structured communication channel exists — without any CS involvement.
Interpretation: Our 10–12% dispute rate was within the normal range for P2P crypto platforms. The problem wasn't dispute frequency — it was the complete absence of a self-resolution layer before CS.
Internal CS data — Pre-launch baseline
All disputes routed directly to CS via ticket. Minimum 2 days to first response. Each dispute required multiple back-and-forth cycles: individual outreach to buyer, individual outreach to seller, clarification loops, approval per risk profile, then fund release. No differentiation between a small payment confirmation and a large fraud escalation.
Interpretation: The system was optimised for nothing. No triage, no self-resolution, no fast path for simple cases. CS time was the only resource — consumed uniformly regardless of dispute size or complexity.
Capacity modelling
At projected P2P volume growth, CS headcount required to maintain existing SLAs would need to increase 3–4x within 18 months. Cost per dispute was trending upward with no ceiling. Hiring was not a strategy — it was a delay.
Interpretation: The only durable solution was infrastructure that resolved the majority of disputes without CS involvement. Headcount scaling is linear. Infrastructure scales better.
The validated pattern

P2P platforms cannot scale dispute volume on CS headcount alone. Without a structured, tiered resolution mechanism — self-resolution first, CS second, platform arbitration last — every new P2P trade is a potential CS ticket. Trust on a P2P marketplace is not built through policy. It is built through infrastructure that users can see working in real time.

06 The Solution — Design Decisions

Cleared — every decision has a reason

None of these were defaults. Each design choice was made against an explicit alternative that was considered and rejected.

Decision
Three-tier escalation, not direct CS routing
Why: Routing all disputes to CS was the status quo — and the ceiling. The first design decision was to introduce a counterparty chat layer (T1) where the majority of disputes — payment confirmations, timing delays, receipt disputes — could self-resolve with no CS involvement at all. CS capacity was freed for disputes that genuinely needed it. T3 platform arbitration was reserved for edge cases only: high-risk profile involvement or deadlocked T2 cases. In practice, T3 was rare.
Decision
Funds held in escrow automatically on dispute initiation — not at CS discretion
Why: The alternative was to hold funds only if CS flagged the trade. This was rejected immediately. Escrow protection had to be automatic the moment a dispute was raised — not discretionary. A dispute by either party triggered an immediate fund block. This removed the incentive for a bad actor to pressure the counterparty outside the platform, because neither party could access the funds regardless of the other's behaviour.
Decision
Mutual agreement in T1 chat → automatic fund release, no CS loop
Why: Manual release approval for every resolved dispute would have put CS back in the loop even for disputes they never needed to touch. If both counterparties confirmed resolution in T1 chat, funds released automatically. This made T1 a genuine resolution pathway — not just a communication channel. The chat log became the source of truth: timestamped, immutable, fully visible to CS on any escalation.
Decision
24h user-triggered escalation, not automatic timer
Why: Auto-escalating to T2 after 24h regardless of chat status would have flooded CS with disputes still in active dialogue. Instead, the user could request CS escalation at any point — and the system auto-triggered T2 if 24 hours elapsed with no resolution and no escalation request. This kept escalation user-driven first, time-bounded second. Any T2 ticket meant T1 had genuinely failed — a clean signal for CS.
Decision
Resolution authority linked to KYC risk profile, not uniform across all users
Why: Treating a fully-verified Tier 3 user identically to an unverified Tier 1 user in a high-value dispute was both a trust failure and a risk failure. The risk profiling engine already governed trade amounts and transaction limits. Dispute resolution was extended into the same framework: KYC tier determined what a user could approve at T1, what required CS countersignature at T2, and what required founder-level sign-off at T3. One trust architecture, not two parallel systems.
Decision
Repeat dispute actors downgraded in the risk engine — not case-managed individually
Why: A user who initiated multiple disputes — particularly disputes that resolved against them — should face structurally lower trade limits and higher scrutiny, not a manual review each time. Dispute outcomes fed back into the risk engine: repeat flagged actors moved to lower tiers, reducing trade limits. In severe cases, accounts were blocked and funds frozen pending review. This made abuse of the dispute system structurally costly, not just inconvenient.
07 System Mechanics

How the tiers worked

Each tier had a defined scope, time boundary, resolution authority, and fund release condition. Every escalation carried a complete evidence trail to the next tier.

Tier 1
Counterparty Chat
0 – 24h · Immediate resolution
Trigger: Either party raises a dispute on a matched P2P trade
Fund status: Automatically blocked in escrow on dispute initiation
Channel: In-platform timestamped chat between buyer and seller only
Resolution: Mutual agreement in chat → automatic fund release. No CS involved.
Escalation: User-requested at any point, or auto-triggered at 24h with no resolution
Coverage: ~90% of disputes — payment confirmations, receipt verification, timing delays
Tier 2
CS Review
24 – 48h from escalation
Trigger: User request or 24h T1 expiry with no mutual resolution
CS access: Full T1 chat log, trade details, both user risk profiles, KYC tier, transaction history
CS action: Reviews evidence, contacts both parties within the platform, reaches a resolution decision
Fund release: CS-approved release once resolved. KYC tier determines whether CS releases unilaterally or requires countersignature.
Escalation: Deadlocked T2 or high-risk profile involvement → T3
Tier 3
Platform Arbitration
36 – 72h · Edge cases only
Trigger: T2 deadlock, high-risk profile on either side, or large fund settlement requiring elevated approval
Decision authority: CEO / founder-level for highest-severity cases. Platform makes a binding decision.
Fund release: Platform-decided — can release to either party, split, or freeze pending further investigation
Outcome: Binding. Results fed back into the risk engine — accounts flagged, trade limits adjusted, accounts blocked if warranted.
Frequency: Rare by design. T1 self-resolution absorbed the majority; T3 existed for genuine edge cases only.
08 Success Metrics

How we knew it was working

Four metrics. Each tied to a specific behaviour the system was designed to move.

90%
CS deflection rate
Post-launch, CS involvement dropped to 10% of disputes — a ~60% reduction in dispute-related support queries overall. The majority of disputes resolved at T1, between counterparties, without any CS touch.
–2 days
Minimum resolution time
Before: all disputes required a minimum 2-day wait before CS could begin. After: T1 disputes resolved immediately on mutual agreement. The 2-day baseline became the upper bound for T2 cases — not the floor for everything.
Tiered
Resolution time by tier
T1: immediate upon agreement. T2 CS resolution: 24–48h from escalation. T3 platform arbitration: 36–72h, rare edge cases only. The system collapsed time for the majority while preserving capacity for complexity.
Zero
Production incidents on launch
Cleared shipped as part of the broader P2P release — one of 4 major platform releases delivered on time with zero production incidents across a platform processing 1K+ daily transactions.
Guardrails — these must not move in the wrong direction
Fund release accuracy: no disputed funds released to the wrong party. Any mis-release is a critical incident regardless of amount.
T1 abuse rate: users gaming the dispute system to delay legitimate trades must not exceed a threshold that undermines counterparty trust in P2P.
CS workload as % of total disputes: must not increase post-launch. If CS is handling more disputes proportionally, T1 is not working as designed.
Risk engine integrity: tier downgrades for repeat dispute actors must be reviewed quarterly to prevent false positives against legitimate traders.
09 Pre-Mortem

What could go wrong

Assume Cleared ships. Six months later it is pulled or rolled back. What happened?

🐯 Tiger — real problem
Dispute system used as a delay mechanism
A bad actor raises a dispute on a legitimate trade — not because there's a real problem, but to hold up fund release while executing another transaction. T1 gives them a 24h window before CS is even triggered. At scale, this becomes a systematic exploit.
Mitigation: Dispute frequency monitoring per user. Repeat actors — especially those whose disputes consistently resolve against them — are flagged in the risk engine and trade limits reduced. Accounts with patterns of bad-faith disputes are blocked. The risk profile feedback loop makes abuse structurally costly.
🐯 Tiger — real problem
T1 chat becomes evidence-fabrication ground
A counterparty uses the T1 chat to create a misleading paper trail — selectively sharing information to manipulate the CS decision at T2. Without immutable logging, CS sees a version of events curated by the bad actor.
Mitigation: T1 chat is timestamped, immutable, and fully visible to CS at T2. Neither party can edit or delete messages. The chat log is the source of truth for all escalation decisions. CS also has access to the full transaction history and KYC profile — not just the chat.
🐘 Elephant — unspoken risk
High-volume sellers lose confidence in platform fairness
If Tier 3 KYC users — the high-volume traders who create P2P liquidity — feel the dispute system is biased against sellers, or that CS decisions are opaque, they exit quietly. No complaint. Just reduced volume, then departure.
Mitigation: T3 decisions are documented with reasoning — both parties receive a decision summary, not just an outcome. High-tier users have access to founder-level escalation as a structural check on CS decision quality. Monitoring T3 repeat actors from the same side is an early warning signal.
🐯 Tiger — manageable
Escrow hold perceived as punishment, not protection
A user whose funds are correctly escrowed during a legitimate payment delay — not a real dispute — perceives the platform as blocking their money. The protection mechanism designed to help them reads as the problem.
Mitigation: Escrow UI must communicate clearly: "Your funds are protected — not frozen." Status updates at each tier change. Estimated resolution time surfaced at T1 and T2. The experience of escrow must feel like safety, not penalty.
10 What I'd Validate Next

The questions shipping would answer — and what we built after

These were the open questions at launch. Several became features in subsequent iterations.

Fraud pattern detection at the dispute level
The risk engine governed trade limits and KYC tiers — but it didn't yet analyse dispute-level patterns: specific transaction types, counterparty combinations, or payment methods that correlated with bad-faith disputes. The next layer was building a fraud signal at the dispute level — what does a weaponised dispute look like before CS notices? This became a subsequent build, layering dispute behaviour signals into the same risk profiling engine that already governed the platform.
Automated resolution for low-value disputes
Not all disputes needed a human in the loop. A payment confirmation dispute between two Tier 3 KYC-verified users, with a matching transaction reference — this could be auto-resolved with high confidence. The question was: what is the right value threshold, risk profile combination, and evidence signal to trigger automated release safely? Defining those parameters and building the automated path was validated and shipped as a subsequent feature.
Seller reputation scoring
P2P liquidity depends on seller reliability. A buyer choosing between two sellers with similar offers has no signal beyond the transaction itself. Dispute history — how often a seller is disputed, how disputes resolve, response time in T1 chat — is exactly the data that powers a reputation layer. This was identified at launch as the next trust-building feature: a seller's dispute record surfaced as a lightweight score visible at the point of trade matching, giving buyers a signal and incentivising sellers to resolve at T1.
T1 resolution rate segmented by dispute type
We tracked CS deflection in aggregate. The next question was: which categories of dispute consistently fail at T1 and escalate to T2? If payment confirmation disputes self-resolve at 95% but "funds not received" cases escalate at 80%, those two dispute types need different T1 tooling — structured evidence templates rather than open chat, or a shorter auto-escalation window. Segmenting resolution rate by dispute category was the data foundation for making T1 smarter over time.
